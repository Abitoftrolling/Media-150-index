Reading and Written Response #1

“In Plato's Cave” from On Photography by Susan Sontag self-reflection

When I think of the current world of photography and how I can “properly” contribute to image making as a whole in the current worldscape we live in, I think of when susan sontag mentions how photographs actively promote nostalgia. Zeroing in on this part of the introduction, the concept of nostalgic moments that are created out of photographs is something that was known to me, but regarded as hated. Photos aren’t something that I personally regarded as “important” in my life because of the sheer amount that my parents would force me to take over the years. As I took more pictures, the significance of photography growing up dwindled as rather than thinking of it as something that could reveal hidden importance to me, It looked more so as a stain in an otherwise perfect moment. Why would you need to take pictures during important moments of your life rather than living them out to the fullest. Why would you interrupt the present of your life to take a snapshot of the current. Unfortunately(or fortunately for people that aren’t in the same boat as me), those snapshots of life define what you become down the road and allow people to look back to fond memories that they had in past events. It's even more important now that AI generation is a thing and pictures of fake events and memories can be created; Memories of things that never happened, memories of events that you weren’t a part of, memories of people that never recognized you. I personally think the best way to contribute to photography is to not make one at all and yet without those photographs being shot, you can falsify your life to make it seem as if you had real experiences even though you haven’t. If I had one reason to help with the world of photography it would be this: I want the removal of false memories. The removal of lies of a life that is unattainable through corrupted images. I would represent through my work a life of pureness. Not pureness like that of a newborn, pureness that allows for mistakes and hardships. Pureness that allows misguidedness and faults in people because without those traits, are we really human?

Another thing Sontag mentions that is related to the point of false memories, is when she speaks on the Korean war, pointing out that Americans would be less inclined to agree with the war if they saw the photos of the destruction of Korea due to the war. This is something that also accords with ai photography(if you can even call it that): false detailing of events. Someone can as easily create an AI image and consider it as fact; in that same vein, anyone can call human art “AI” to undermine its importance as fact. Although one approach is to blame the general populace for continuously falling for these lies, the continuous improvement of ai has to shoulder the blame as well. Overall, we cannot blame the population for not creating memories on their own and relying on AI to do it for them. Instead of criticizing, we can instead learn to help them with photographs of our own to teach them the value of the picture.
